About Schistura Hiranyakeshi


Classification / Names:
Teleostei (teleosts) > Cypriniformes (Carps) > Nemacheilidae (Brook loaches).


Scientific name:
Schistura hiranyakeshi Praveenraj, Thackeray & Shankar 2020.


Common name:
Hiranyakeshi Loach.


History:
Discovery:
Tejas Thackeray and his team discovered a new freshwater fish species in the Western Ghats, specifically near Amboli in Sawantwadi Tehsil of Sindhudurga district, Maharashtra. The fish was first spotted in the pond at the Mahadev temple. Named as Schistura hiranyakeshi, the name is derived from its subspecies Schistura and the nearby Hiranyakeshi River. Maharashtra government declared the region as a biodiversity heritage site to conserve the species.


Distribution:
Endemic to the Temple pond or 'Kund" which is at the origin of the Hinanyakeshi River, Amboli, Sindhudurga.


Etymology:
Schistura: Greek, schizein = to divide + Greek, oura = tail; an allusion to forked caudal fins hiranyakeshi: Named for the place of its type locality, Hiranyakeshi River, India. The species is named after the Hiranyakeshi River in Sindhudurga District of Maharashtra. The epithet "hinanyakeshi" in Sanskrit means golden hair. Further, this also alludes to the golden-yellow coloration and stripes found on the body of adult specimens.


Diagnosis:
This species is distinguished from its congeners in the peninsular, northeastern, and central India, and Sri Lanka by the following characters: an incomplete lateral line with 6-7 pores, ending at a point vertical at half the length of the adpressed pectoral fin; dorsal fin and caudal fin without spots or blotches; body with 9-10 bars that are wider or almost equal in width to the interspaces; dorsal fin, anal fin and sub-dorsal bars have a unique crimson color in adult males; live and preserved specimens have the lower lip with a black mark on each side of the median interruption; no suborbital flap or axillary pelvic lobe


Importance of the area:
The 2.11-hectare area near Amboli becomes the 5th biodiversity heritage site (BHS) in Maharashtra. Other BHS in Maharashtra include Glory of Allapalli, Landor Khori Park, Ganesh Khind, and the Myristica swamp in Sindhudurga.The fish Schistura hiranyakeshi was spotted for the first time in a pond at the Mahadev Temple at Amboli in Sindhudurga district , Western Ghats . The fish was found by the team led by Tejas Thackeray, a wildlife researcher, and son of Maharashtra CM Uddhav Thackeray. The Hiranyakeshi river originates from inside the temple caves with its waters flowing towards Jakatwadi, a small village near Amboli. The Temple pond or Kund to which this species is endemic to is located at the origin of this river, Researchers have conducted surveys across the landscape to identify new locations for this species, however, it was found that the Temple: pond is the only stronghold of this species. The researchers did not identify a single new population outside the town of Amboli in Sindhudurga where the species can be found in good numbers. Further, the area is also considered a "Devrai" or "Sacred Groves" by the villagers since generations. Which makes this not only a special location because the species endemic nature but also because it is considered a holy ground for the local community.


Diversity:
Description of Schistura hiranyakeshi as a freshwater loach, belonging to the superfamily Cobitoidea. Native habitat of Schistura hiranyakeshi in Eurasia and Northern Africa.


Ecology Habitats:
Schistura hiranyakeshi inhabits freshwater environments with high oxygen levels.


Conservation Biodiversity Heritage Site:
The Maharashtra Government took its first step to set up the "Schistura hiranyakeshi Biodiversity Heritage site on 31st March 2021. This was done with a view to save this newly described freshwater fish, this makes initiative a first of its kind in the country which is wholly dedicated to a lesser-known species. The local inhabitants have long considered this fish to be a "divine fish" which protects the villagers from evil forces prevalent outside The villagers have committed to protect this species from extinction. To: honour this commitment, the villagers have joined hands with scientists, researchers and local NGOs in creating a unique "Temple Community Conservation model for this freshwater fish, This unique model will set a precedent, to showcase that with little effort from the local community backed by scientific research and willing government, will slowly but surely lead to an overall conservation of this sacred species


Threats:
Potential pressure from Ornamental fish trade and any damage to its habitat may push this highly endemic species to the brink of extinction.


What can you do:

  • Do not litter the temple premises, use the trashcans.
  • Do not pollute the holy temple pond.
  • Do not feed the fish.
  • Do not catch the fish.
  • If you see anyone catching the fish or engaging in activities that could damage the habitat, please report it to the local forest department.
